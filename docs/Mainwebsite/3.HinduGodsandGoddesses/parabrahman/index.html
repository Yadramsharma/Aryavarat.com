The Supreme Reality in Sanatan Dharma: Parabrahman

Chapter 1: The Nature of Parabrahman

1.1 Introduction:
 In the eternal philosophical tradition of Sanatan Dharma, the concept of Parabrahman stands as the highest spiritual ideal.
The term "Parabrahman" is derived from two Sanskrit words: Para (supreme, beyond) and Brahman (the infinite, unchanging reality)
Parabrahman is not simply a deity among many; it is the Absolute Reality, beyond form, name, time, and causation.
Parabrahman is both Nirguna(without attributes) and Saguna (with attributes), manifesting both as formless infinity and as the deities worshipped in temples and scriptures.
In essence, Parabrahman transcends all conceptual limitations, serving as the source and substratum of the universe.

1.2 Parabrahman in the Vedas
The Vedas, regarded as the oldest scriptural texts in Hinduism, offer some of the earliest insights into Parabrahman.
In Rigveda and other Samhitas, the Supreme is described indirectly through cosmic metaphors.

Rigveda (10.129.1–7) – Nasadiya Sukta:

श्लोक
नासद् आसीन् नो सद् आसीत् तदानीं नासीद् रजो नो व्योमा परो यत् |
किम् आवरीवः कुह कस्य शर्मन्न् अम्भः किम् आसीद् गहनं गभीरम् ||

shloka
nāsad āsīn no sad āsīt tadānīṃ nāsīd rajo no vyomā paro yat | kim āvarīvaḥ kuha kasya śarmann ambhaḥ kim āsīd gahanaṃ gabhīram ||

Translation: Then, there was neither non-existence nor existence. There was no realm of air, no sky beyond it. What covered it, and where? What gave shelter? Was there water, deep and unfathomable?

This hymn reflects the ineffable and mysterious nature of the Supreme before creation. Parabrahman is beyond dualities such as existence and non-existence.


Yajurveda (32.3)

श्लोक
एको देवो नित्यो लोकेषु गूढः

shloka
eko devo nityo lokeshu gudhah

Translation: There is only one deity, eternal, hidden in all beings.

This verse refers to the singular, all-pervading presence of the Supreme.



1.3 Parabrahman in the Upanishads
The Upanishads provide a more refined and introspective inquiry into the nature of Parabrahman.

Brihadaranyaka Upanishad (1.4.10)

श्लोक
अहं ब्रह्मास्मि

Shloka
Ahaṁ Brahmāsmi

Translation: I am Brahman.

This mahavakya (great saying) asserts the non-dual identity of the individual self (Atman) with the Supreme Self (Parabrahman).


Chandogya Upanishad (6.8.7)

श्लोक
"तत् त्वम् असि"

Shloka
Tat tvam asi

Translation: That Thou Art.

It proclaims that the essence of the self is identical with the essence of the universe.


Taittiriya Upanishad (2.1.1)

श्लोक
सत्यं ज्ञानं अनन्तं ब्रह्म

Shloka
Satyam Jnanam Anantam Brahma

Translation: Brahman is Truth, Knowledge, and Infinity.

These expressions reveal the inner nature of Parabrahman as infinite existence, awareness, and bliss (Sat-Chit-Ananda).



1.4 Parabrahman in Bhagavad Gita

Bhagavad Gita – Chapter 8, Verse 3

श्लोक
श्रीभगवान उवाच: अक्षरं ब्रह्म परमं स्वभावोऽध्यात्मं उच्यते

Shloka
śhrī bhagavān uvācha akṣharaṁ brahma paramaṁ svabhāvo ’dhyātmam uchyate bhūta-bhāvodbhava-karo visargaḥ karma-sanjñitaḥ

Translation: The Supreme Indestructible is called Brahman; its nature is spiritual.


Bhagavad Gita – Chapter 10, Verse 12

श्लोक
परं ब्रह्म परं धाम पवित्रं परमं भवान् । पुरुषं शाश्वतं दिव्यमादिदेवमजं विभुम् ॥

Shloka
paraṃ brahma paraṃ dhāma pavitraṃ paramaṃ bhavān puruṣaṃ śāśvataṃ divyamādidevamajaṃ vibhum

Translation: You are the Supreme Brahman, the Supreme Abode, the Purest; the Eternal Divine Person, the Primal God, the Unborn, and the All-pervading (Omnipresent) One.

These verses reveal Parabrahman's divine nature as both the supreme goal and the innermost Self.

1.5 Parabrahman in the Ramayana
In the Ramayana, Lord Rama is depicted not merely as a prince, but as Parabrahman incarnate.

Valmiki Ramayana – Yuddha Kanda (117.13)

श्लोक
रामम् विधि परम्ब्रह्म सच्चिदानन्द विग्रहम्

Shloka
Raam Viddhi Parabrahma Sachchidananda Vigraham

Translation: Know Rama to be the Supreme Brahman, the embodiment of Existence, Consciousness, and Bliss.

This identification reinforces the Vedantic interpretation of divine avatars.

1.6 Parabrahman in the Puranas
The Puranas abound with stories, hymns, and philosophy illustrating the glories of Parabrahman through forms like Vishnu, Shiva, and Devi.

Vishnu Purana (1.2.10)

श्लोक
इको वासिं परं ब्रह्म नान्यद यत् सत्-असत् परम्

shloka
eko vāsiṁ paraṁ brahma nānyad yat sat-asat param

Translation: There is only one Supreme Brahman, the ultimate reality beyond existence and non-existence.

Devi Bhagavata Purana

श्लोक
देवी ह्येष जगत्-सर्वं ब्रह्मरूप सनातनी

shloka
ī hy eṣā jagat-sarvaṁ brahma-rūpā sanātanī

Translation: The Goddess is the whole universe, the eternal form of Brahman.

These texts affirm that the Supreme can manifest in multiple divine forms while remaining beyond all.


Chapter 2: The Journey of Ātman to Parabrahman

2.1 Introduction:

In the vast metaphysical framework of Sanatan Dharma, the journey of the Ātman (soul) toward Parabrahman (Supreme Reality) is the ultimate path of spiritual realization.
This path transcends religious rituals and dogma, guiding seekers inward to the unchanging truth that lies within themselves.
The Upanishadic declarations like "Tat Tvam Asi" (You are That) and "Aham Brahmāsmi" (I am Brahman) serve as compasses in this inner journey.
2.2 The Nature of Ātman
The Ātman is described in Hindu scriptures as eternal, unchanging, indivisible, and pure consciousness. It is never born, nor does it die.

Katha Upanishad (2.18)

श्लोक
न जायते म्रियते वा कदाचिन् नायं भूत्वा भविता वा न भूयः।
अजो नित्यः शाश्वतोऽयं पुराणो न हन्यते हन्यमाने शरीरे॥

Shloka
na jāyate mriyate vā kadācin nāyaṁ bhūtvā bhavitā vā na bhūyaḥ.
ajo nityaḥ śāśvato'yaṁ purāṇo na hanyate hanyamāne śarīre.

Translation: "The soul is never born, nor does it die; it is unborn, eternal, ever-existing, and primeval. It is not slain when the body is slain."
This verse from the Katha Upanishad describes the eternal nature of the soul (Ātman). It asserts that the soul is never created nor destroyed, unaffected by birth or death. Even when the body is destroyed, the soul remains untouched — highlighting the core Sanātan Dharma principle of immortality of the self.
2.3 Ignorance and the Veil(mask) of Māyā
According to Vedanta, the root cause of bondage is avidyā (ignorance).
It veils the true nature of the Ātman and projects the illusion of separateness.

Svetasvatara Upanishad (4.10)

श्लोक
अजामेकां लोहितशुक्लकृष्णां ह्वीः प्रजाः सृजमानां सरूपाः।
अजो ह्येको जुषमाणोऽनुशेते जहात्येनां भुक्तभोगामजोऽन्यः||

Shloka
ajāmekāṁ lohitaśuklakṛṣṇāṁ bahvīḥ prajāḥ sṛjamānāṁ sarūpāḥ |
ajo hyeko juṣamāṇo'nuśete jahātyenāṁ bhuktabhogām ajo'nyaḥ ||

Translation: "The one unborn being gives birth to many forms, colored red, white, and black. Another unborn being enjoys her. A third unborn being leaves her after enjoying."
This profound verse refers to Prakriti (Nature) and Purusha (Soul). - The unborn female (Ajā) creates all beings and manifests in red (rajas), white (sattva), and black (tamas) qualities. - One unborn being enjoys her — symbolizing the bound soul. - Another unborn being transcends her — symbolizing the liberated soul who has realized the Self and renounced worldly pleasures.
2.4 The Path of Self-Realization
The Vedic path offers various approaches to reach Parabrahman: Yoga (Path of Knowledge), Bhakti Yoga (Devotion), Karma Yoga (Action), and Raja Yoga (Meditation).

Bhagavad Gita (6.29)

श्लोक
सर्वभूतस्थमात्मानं सर्वभूतानि चात्मनि ।
ईक्षते योगयुक्तात्मा सर्वत्र समदर्शन: ॥

Shloka
sarva-bhūta-stham ātmānaṁ sarva-bhūtāni cātmani |
īkṣate yoga-yuktātmā sarvatra sama-darśanaḥ ||

Translation:
"The yogi sees the Self present in all beings and all beings in the Self; he sees the same everywhere."
2.5 The Realization of Parabrahman
When the veil of ignorance is lifted, the individual Ātman recognizes its true identity as Parabrahman.

Mandukya Upanishad (Verse 7)

श्लोक
नान्तःप्रज्ञं न बहिष्प्रज्ञं नोभयतःप्रज्ञं न प्रज्ञानघनं न प्रज्ञं नाप्रज्ञम्‌।
अदृष्टमव्यवहार्यमग्राह्यमलक्षणमचिन्त्यमव्यपदेश्यमेकात्मप्रत्ययसारं प्रपञ्चोपशमं शान्तं शिवमद्वैतं चतुर्थं मन्यन्ते स आत्मा स विज्ञेयः ॥

Shloka
na antaḥ-prajñaṁ na bahiḥ-prajñaṁ nobhayataḥ-prajñaṁ na prajñāna-ghanam na prajñaṁ nāprajñam |
adṛṣṭam avyavahāryam agrāhyam alakṣaṇam acintyam avyapadeśyam ekātma-pratyaya-sāram prapañcopaśamam śāntaṁ śivam advaitaṁ caturthaṁ manyante sa ātmā sa vijñeyaḥ ||

Translation:
"It is not that which is conscious of the inner world, nor that which is conscious of the outer world... It is peace, it is bliss, it is non-dual. This is the Self to be realized."
2.6 Liberation (Mokṣa)
When Ātman realizes its unity with Parabrahman, the cycle of birth and death ceases. This is known as Mokṣa—liberation.

Bhagavad Gita (2.72)

श्लोक

एषा ब्राह्मी स्थितिः पार्थ नैनां प्राप्य विमुह्यति।
स्थित्वास्यामन्तकालेऽपि ब्रह्मनिर्वाणमृच्छति॥

eṣā brāhmī sthitiḥ pārtha naināṁ prāpya vimuhyati
sthitvāsyām anta-kāle 'pi brahma-nirvāṇam ṛcchati

Translation: "This is the Brahmic state, O Partha. Having attained this, one is never deluded. Being established in this state, even at the hour of death, one attains Brahman."
This verse from the Bhagavad Gita explains the ultimate state of realization (Brāhmī Sthiti) where one is free from delusion. It asserts that even at the time of death, one established in this consciousness attains union with Brahman — the eternal, formless Absolute.

Chapter 3: Parabrahman in the Itihasas and Epics

3.1 Introduction
The two great epics of Hindu tradition—Ramayana and Mahabharata—offer profound revelations of the Supreme Reality known as Parabrahman.
While epic in their narrative scope, they are also deeply philosophical, depicting the Supreme Being manifesting in divine incarnations to guide the world and uphold Dharma.
3.2 Parabrahman in the Ramayana
The Ramayana, composed by Maharishi Valmiki, portrays Lord Rama as the embodiment of Parabrahman. Though appearing as a mortal prince, Rama’s divine nature is emphasized in several key verses.

Yuddha Kanda (117.13)

श्लोक

रामं विद्धि परंब्रह्म सच्चिदानंद विग्रहम्॥

rāmaṁ vidhi parambrahma saccidānanda vigraham

Translation: “Know Rama to be the Supreme Brahman, the embodiment of Existence, Consciousness, and Bliss (Sat-Chit-Ananda).”
This śloka directly affirms that Rama is not merely a divine king or an ideal man, but the eternal, formless Supreme Truth — Sat-Chit-Ananda, the indivisible essence of reality, bliss, and consciousness.

Ayodhya Kanda (1.7.10)

श्लोक

रामो विग्रहवान् धर्मः॥

rāmo vigrahavān dharmaḥ

Translation: “Rama is Dharma personified.”
The above line reveals the truth that Dharma is not abstract but incarnate as Parabrahman in Rama’s form. He is the living embodiment of cosmic righteousness — the divine ideal in action.

Vishnu Sahasranama {Mahabharata (Anushasana Parva)}

श्लोक

राम राघव राजेति रामो रामेति मे मनः॥

Shloka
rāma rāghava rājeti rāmo rāmeti me manah

Meaning: Parabrahman chooses to descend as Rama to demonstrate the highest virtues and protect cosmic balance.
This verse from the Vishnu Sahasranama captures the meditative love and reverence for Rama. Repeating his name is not only devotion but also remembrance of the eternal truth embodied in Parabrahman's divine form.
3.3 Parabrahman in the Mahabharata
The Mahabharata , composed by Ved Vyasa, contains the Bhagavad Gita , a direct conversation between Krishna (Parabrahman) and Arjuna (the soul in confusion).

Bhagavad Gita (10.20)

श्लोक

अहं आत्मा गुडाकेश सर्वभूताशयस्थितः ।
अहम् आदिश्च मध्यं च भूतानामन्त एव च ॥

Shloka
aham ātmā guḍākeśa sarva-bhūtāśaya-sthitaḥ |
aham ādiś ca madhyaṁ ca bhūtānām anta eva ca

Translation: “I am the Self, O Gudakesha, seated in the hearts of all beings. I am the beginning, the middle, and the end of all beings.”

Bhagavad Gita (11.38)

श्लोक

त्वमक्षरं परं वेदितव्यं
त्वमस्य विश्वस्य परं निधानम्॥

Shloka
tvam akṣaraṁ paramaṁ veditavyaṁ
tvam asya viśvasya paraṁ nidhānam

Translation: “You are the imperishable, the Supreme to be realized; you are the final resting place of all the universe.”
Krishna reveals his Vishvarupa (Cosmic Form) in Chapter 11, showing he is not limited to one form but the infinite totality — the eternal substratum of all existence, beyond birth and decay.

Mahabharata, Udyoga Parva (93.24)

श्लोक

कृष्णस्तु भगवान्स्वयम्॥

Shloka
krishnas tu bhagavān svayam

Translation: “Krishna is the Supreme Lord Himself.”
This concise statement confirms that Krishna is not just a divine incarnation, but **Parabrahman in totality** — the origin, sustainer, and ultimate reality behind all creation and cosmic order.


Chapter 4: Parabrahman in Puranas and Agamas

4.1 Introduction
The Puranas and Agamas elaborate on cosmic theology, divine leelas (plays), creation, and dissolution.
Parabrahman is described in countless divine forms such as Vishnu, Shiva, Devi, and more, while always being beyond form and attributes.
4.2 Vishnu Purana

Vishnu Purana (1.2.10):

श्लोक
एको वासिन् परं ब्रह्म |
नान्यत् यत् सत्-असत् परम् ||

shloka
eko vāsiṁ paraṁ brahma|
nānyad yat sat-asat param ||

Translation: There is only one Supreme Brahman, the highest reality beyond existence and non-existence.

Vishnu is shown as the supreme aspect of Parabrahman who takes multiple forms for the sake of creation, preservation, and destruction.

Vishnu Purana (1.2.10)

श्लोक

एको वासिं परं ब्रह्म नान्यद् यत् सत्-असत् परम्॥

Shloka
eko vāsiṁ paraṁ brahma nānyad yat sat-asat param

Translation: “There is only one Supreme Brahman, the highest reality beyond existence and non-existence.”
Vishnu is described as the supreme manifestation of Parabrahman — the One beyond dualities. He assumes countless forms to sustain creation, uphold dharma, and ultimately dissolve illusion.
4.3 Shiva Purana

Shiva Purana (Vidhyeshvara Samhita)

श्लोक

शिवात्मकम् इदं विश्वम्॥

Shloka
śivātmakam idaṁ viśvaṁ

Translation: “This entire universe is the very form of Shiva.”
Here, Shiva is identified as Parabrahman — not separate from the cosmos, but the very fabric of it. He is both the manifest and the formless Absolute, the silent source of all that exists.
4.4 Devi Bhagavata Purana

Devi Bhagavata (1.3.2)

श्लोक

देवी ह्येषा जगत्सर्वं ब्रह्मरूपा सनातनी॥

Shloka
devī hy eṣā jagat-sarvaṁ brahma-rūpā sanātanī

Translation: “The Goddess is this entire universe, the eternal form of Brahman.”
This verse expands the understanding of Parabrahman into the feminine — the **Sanātanī**, the eternal divine mother, showing that the Absolute is not limited by gender, form, or name. She is creation itself.
4.5 Agamas
Agamas provide rituals and temple architecture but also declare the oneness of all forms with the formless Brahman.
In Shaiva, Vaishnava, and Shakta Agamas alike, the Supreme is worshipped as the ultimate cause and aim of all existence.


Chapter 5: Realization through Yogic Paths

5.1 Introduction
Sanatan Dharma emphasizes direct realization of Parabrahman through paths suitable for various temperaments— Jnana (knowledge), Bhakti (devotion), Karma (action), and Raja (meditation). All these converge on the same non-dual truth.
5.2 Jnana Yoga (Path of Knowledge)

Mandukya Upanishad

श्लोक

अयम् आत्मा ब्रह्म॥

Shloka
ayam ātmā brahma

Translation: “This Self is Brahman.”
Through study, discrimination, and meditation, one sheds illusion (Maya) and directly knows the **Self (Ātman)** as non-different from the **Supreme Reality (Brahman)** — the indivisible truth of all existence.
5.3 Bhakti Yoga (Path of Devotion)

Bhagavad Gita (9.22)

श्लोक

अनन्याश्चिन्तयन्तो मां ये जना: पर्युपासते
तेषां नित्याभियुक्तानां योगक्षेमं वहाम्यहम्॥

Shloka
ananyāś cintayanto māṁ ye janāḥ paryupāsate
teṣāṁ nityābhiyuktānāṁ yoga-kṣemaṁ vahāmy aham

Translation: “To those who are devoted to Me with a single-minded focus, I provide what they lack and preserve what they have.”
Through loving devotion and unwavering remembrance of the Divine in personal form, the devotee becomes inwardly absorbed. The Lord ensures their spiritual and material needs, drawing them ever closer to the infinite.
5.4 Karma Yoga (Path of Action)

Bhagavad Gita (3.19)

श्लोक

तस्मादसक्तः सततं कार्यं कर्म समाचर॥

Shloka
tasmād asaktaḥ satataṁ kāryaṁ karma samācara

Translation: “Therefore, perform your duty without attachment.”
Detached action, performed as duty without craving for results, purifies the mind and helps dissolve ego. It opens the path to inner freedom and Self-realization.
5.5 Raja Yoga (Path of Meditation)

Yoga Sutras of Patanjali (1.2)

सूत्र

योगश् चित्तवृत्तिनिरोधः॥

Sutra
yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ

Translation: “Yoga is the cessation of mental fluctuations.”
When the ever-changing movements of the mind are silenced, the true Self is revealed — calm, luminous, and non-different from **Parabrahman**, the ultimate reality.
5.6 Integration and Liberation
Sanatan Dharma does not see these as mutually exclusive paths. In fact, a balanced life often integrates all.
Realization of Parabrahman is moksha—liberation from the cycle of birth and death.

Bhagavad Gita (4.24)

श्लोक

ब्रह्मार्पणं ब्रह्म हविर्ब्रह्माग्नौ ब्रह्मणा हुतम् ।
ब्रह्मैव तेन गन्तव्यं ब्रह्मकर्मसमाधिना ॥

Shloka
brahmārpaṇaṁ brahma havir brahmāgnau brahmaṇā hutam
brahmaiva tena gantavyaṁ brahma-karma-samādhinā

Translation: “The act of offering is Brahman; the offering itself is Brahman; it is offered by Brahman into the fire of Brahman. He who sees Brahman in every action attains Brahman.”
This verse reveals the ultimate unity in all ritual and action. When performed with knowledge and meditation, every act becomes an offering to the Self — the eternal Brahman — and the doer merges into that oneness.
5.7 Final Reflection
Parabrahman is not a distant reality but your own true nature. All scriptures, all deities, all yogas lead to one truth: Tvam Asi – You are That.
